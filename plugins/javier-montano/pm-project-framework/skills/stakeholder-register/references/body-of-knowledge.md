# Body of Knowledge — Stakeholder Register & Analysis

> Academic, industrial, and practitioner sources for stakeholder identification, power/interest analysis, engagement planning, and register management.

---

## 1. Foundational Frameworks

### PMBOK 7th Edition — Stakeholder Performance Domain

- **Relevance:** PMI dedicates an entire performance domain to stakeholders, recognizing that stakeholder engagement is a primary determinant of project success. The domain covers identification, understanding, analysis, prioritization, engagement, and monitoring.
- **Key concepts:** Stakeholder engagement assessment matrix, power/interest grid, influence/impact grid, salience model (power, legitimacy, urgency).
- **Use in skill:** The 10-step protocol maps directly to PMBOK's stakeholder management processes. Power/interest matrix is the primary analysis tool.
- **Source:** PMI (2021). *PMBOK Guide — 7th Edition*.

### Mitchell, Agle & Wood — Stakeholder Salience Model

- **Relevance:** The salience model classifies stakeholders by three attributes: power (ability to impose will), legitimacy (appropriateness of involvement), and urgency (time sensitivity of claims). Stakeholders possessing all three attributes are "definitive" and require immediate attention.
- **Key insight:** Stakeholders with only one attribute (dormant, discretionary, demanding) can quickly gain additional attributes. Dormant stakeholders with power can become dangerous if they gain urgency.
- **Use in skill:** Use salience model for complex stakeholder landscapes (>20 stakeholders) where power/interest alone is insufficient.
- **Source:** Mitchell, R.K., Agle, B.R. & Wood, D.J. (1997). *Toward a Theory of Stakeholder Identification and Salience*.

### Mendelow's Power/Interest Matrix

- **Relevance:** The foundational 2x2 matrix for stakeholder prioritization. Classifies stakeholders into four quadrants based on power and interest levels.

| | Low Interest | High Interest |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power** | Keep Satisfied | Manage Closely |
| **Low Power** | Monitor | Keep Informed |

- **Strategy per quadrant:**
  - **Manage Closely:** Regular face-to-face meetings, involve in decisions, proactive updates
  - **Keep Satisfied:** Address concerns promptly, brief before public announcements, avoid surprises
  - **Keep Informed:** Regular status reports, newsletters, steering committee minutes
  - **Monitor:** Periodic check on position changes, minimal active engagement

- **Source:** Mendelow, A. (1991). *Stakeholder Mapping*.

## 2. Engagement Assessment

### Engagement Level Definitions

| Level | Description | Indicators |
|-------|------------|------------|
| **Unaware** | Not aware of the project or its impact | No communication received, not in distribution list |
| **Resistant** | Aware and opposed to the project | Vocal opposition, blocking behaviors, refusal to participate |
| **Neutral** | Aware but neither supportive nor opposed | Passive, attends meetings when required, provides minimal input |
| **Supportive** | Aware and supportive of the project | Active participation, provides resources, advocates for project |
| **Leading** | Actively championing the project | Drives adoption, removes obstacles, advocates to leadership |

### Engagement Gap Analysis

For each stakeholder, document:
- **Current engagement level** (C) — based on observed behavior
- **Desired engagement level** (D) — what the project needs from them
- **Gap** (D - C) — the engagement delta
- **Strategy** — specific actions to close the gap

Gaps of 2+ levels require intensive engagement strategy and may require sponsor intervention.

## 3. Stakeholder Communication Preferences

### Communication Matrix Template

| Stakeholder | Preferred Channel | Frequency | Format | Detail Level | Timing Sensitivity |
|-------------|------------------|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Executive sponsor | 1:1 briefing | Bi-weekly | Verbal + 1-page summary | Strategic, RAG only | Before board meetings |
| Functional managers | Email + meeting | Weekly | Status report | Operational detail | End of week |
| End users | Town hall | Monthly | Presentation | Impact-focused | After sprint reviews |
| Regulators | Formal report | Quarterly | Compliance report | Full detail | Before audit periods |

### Communication Principles

1. **No surprises rule:** High-power stakeholders hear bad news from the PM before they hear it from anyone else
2. **Channel match:** Use the stakeholder's preferred channel, not the PM's preferred channel
3. **Frequency calibration:** Over-communication is better than under-communication for high-power stakeholders
4. **Format adaptation:** Executives need 1-page summaries; technical leads need detailed data

## 4. Political Dynamics

### Organizational Politics Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Impact on Project | Management Strategy |
|---------|------------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Turf war** | Departments competing for ownership | Scope disputes, resource hoarding | Clear RACI, sponsor arbitration |
| **Shadow power** | Influencer without formal authority | Informal veto power over decisions | Identify and engage early |
| **Passive resistance** | Agreeing in meetings, blocking in practice | Delayed deliverables, quality issues | Track commitments, escalate patterns |
| **Coalition building** | Groups forming alliances for/against project | Amplified support or opposition | Identify coalitions, engage leaders |
| **Information asymmetry** | Some stakeholders know more than others | Trust erosion, surprise opposition | Equalize information through communication plan |

### Resistance Management Approaches

| Resistance Type | Approach | Timeline |
|----------------|----------|----------|
| Rational (disagrees with logic) | Present evidence and data | 1-2 meetings |
| Emotional (fears impact) | Acknowledge concerns, demonstrate benefits | 2-4 weeks |
| Political (protects position) | Find win-win, engage through sponsor | 4-8 weeks |
| Cultural (contradicts values) | Align project messaging with organizational values | Ongoing |
