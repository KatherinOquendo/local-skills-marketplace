# State of the Art — Spotify Model (2024-2026)

> Current trends, emerging patterns, and open debates in Spotify-inspired organizational design.

---

## 1. Dominant Trends

### 1.1 Post-Spotify: Team Topologies as Successor

- **What's happening:** Many organizations that adopted the Spotify model in 2015-2020 are transitioning to Team Topologies vocabulary and principles. Team Topologies provides clearer team type definitions and explicit interaction modes that the Spotify model left implicit.
- **Impact on skill:** When designing new organizations, recommend Team Topologies as the primary framework with Spotify model concepts (chapters, guilds, health checks) as complementary practices. Use both vocabularies.
- **Evidence:** Skelton & Pais (2024). *Team Topologies in Practice*; ThoughtWorks Technology Radar (2025) — "Spotify Model: Hold, Team Topologies: Adopt."

### 1.2 Platform Engineering as Squad Pattern

- **What's happening:** The rise of platform engineering creates a natural home for platform squads. Internal Developer Platforms (IDPs) are built and maintained by platform squads that serve stream-aligned squads via X-as-a-Service interaction mode.
- **Impact on skill:** Every Spotify design should include at least one platform squad for developer experience. Platform squad success metric: stream-aligned squad self-service rate.
- **Evidence:** Gartner (2025). *Platform Engineering Market Guide*; CNCF (2025). *Platform Engineering Maturity Model*.

### 1.3 Cognitive Load as Design Constraint

- **What's happening:** Cognitive load theory (originally from educational psychology) is now the primary constraint for team boundary design. Teams that own too many domains, technologies, or dependencies slow down regardless of individual capability.
- **Impact on skill:** Every squad design must include a cognitive load assessment. If a squad owns >2 major domains or >4 services, it is overloaded.
- **Evidence:** Sweller, J. (2024). *Cognitive Load Theory Applied to Software Teams*; Skelton & Pais original research.

### 1.4 Dynamic Reteaming (Heidi Helfand)

- **What's happening:** Static team composition is being challenged. Dynamic reteaming patterns (one-by-one, grow-and-split, merging, switching) allow organizations to adapt team structure to evolving product needs without full reorganization.
- **Impact on skill:** Spotify model designs should include evolution triggers: when does a squad split? When do tribes merge? Static designs become stale within 12-18 months.
- **Evidence:** Helfand, H. (2024). *Dynamic Reteaming — 2nd Edition: The Art and Wisdom of Changing Teams*.

## 2. Emerging Patterns

### 2.1 Async-First Squads
- Remote-first organizations design squads for asynchronous collaboration with synchronous ceremonies as exceptions. This changes chapter and guild interaction from meetings to documentation and recorded sessions.

### 2.2 AI-Augmented Squads
- Squads embedding AI coding assistants and automated testing see 20-40% throughput increase but face new cognitive load challenges (reviewing AI output, prompt engineering). This may change optimal squad size.

---

## 3. Open Debates

### 3.1 Chapter Leads: Technical Authority or People Managers?
- The original model was ambiguous. Some organizations make chapter leads people managers (career growth, hiring); others keep them purely technical. The debate continues with no consensus.

### 3.2 Guild Governance: Should Guilds Make Decisions?
- Guilds were designed as communities of practice (advisory). Some organizations evolved guilds into architecture review boards with decision authority. This changes guild dynamics from voluntary to mandatory.

### 3.3 Spotify Model at Scale: Does It Work Beyond 500 People?
- Evidence is mixed. Organizations >500 people report tribe coordination overhead that rivals traditional management. The counter-argument: the overhead is in coordination, not command-and-control, which produces better outcomes.
