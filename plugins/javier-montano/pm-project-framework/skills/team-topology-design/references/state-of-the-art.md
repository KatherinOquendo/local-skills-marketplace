# State of the Art — Team Topology Design (2024-2026)

> Current trends, emerging patterns, and open debates in team structure design.

---

## 1. Dominant Trends

### 1.1 Platform Engineering as Organizational Priority

- **What's happening:** Platform engineering is the fastest-growing team pattern. Internal Developer Platforms (IDPs) built by platform teams are becoming essential infrastructure for engineering organizations with 50+ developers.
- **Impact on skill:** Every team topology design for medium+ organizations should include at least one platform team. Platform team investment is a strategic decision.
- **Evidence:** Gartner (2025). *80% of software engineering organizations will establish platform teams by 2026*.

### 1.2 Dynamic Team Boundaries

- **What's happening:** Static team structures are being replaced by dynamic boundaries that evolve with product architecture. When a service is split, the team may split. When services converge, teams may merge.
- **Impact on skill:** Design team topologies with explicit evolution triggers: when does a team split? merge? change type?
- **Evidence:** Helfand, H. (2024). *Dynamic Reteaming — 2nd Edition*.

### 1.3 AI Impact on Team Size

- **What's happening:** AI coding assistants increase individual developer productivity by 30-40%, potentially enabling smaller teams or teams with broader scope. The optimal team size may shift from 5-9 to 4-7 for AI-augmented teams.
- **Impact on skill:** Factor AI tooling into cognitive load assessment. AI reduces extraneous cognitive load (boilerplate, documentation) but does not reduce intrinsic load (domain complexity).
- **Evidence:** GitHub (2025). *Copilot Enterprise Impact Study*.

---

## 2. Open Debates

### 2.1 Team Topologies vs. Spotify Model
- Team Topologies provides clearer team type definitions; Spotify Model provides better cultural practices (chapters, guilds, health checks). The emerging consensus: use both. Team Topologies for structure, Spotify practices for culture.

### 2.2 How Small Can a Platform Team Be?
- Platform teams of 2-3 people are common but controversial. Critics argue they cannot provide reliable, self-service platforms. Advocates argue that starting small and growing based on demand is more efficient than over-investing early.
