# Rubric Criteria: Detailed Scoring Guide

This document provides the detailed evaluation framework for the 10 criteria. Use this guide when scoring content or diagnosing why a criterion received a particular score.

---

## 1. Fundamento (Foundation/Structural Completeness)

**Measures:** Does the response have all necessary structural components? Are all sections present and logically ordered?

### Score 10: Excellence
- Complete structural integrity: all necessary sections present and well-organized
- Clear hierarchical progression from foundation to conclusion
- No missing pieces that would require the reader to seek external context
- Introduction, body, and conclusion/call-to-action all present
- Section count and depth match the topic's complexity

### Score 8: Good
- Most necessary sections present; 1-2 minor gaps
- Logical flow is clear but could be slightly refined
- Reader can follow the argument without significant external research
- Structure supports the main claims effectively

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Core sections present but some auxiliary sections missing
- Structure is functional but somewhat uneven
- Readers might need to infer connections between sections
- Introduction or conclusion could be stronger

### Score 4: Weak
- Multiple sections missing or unclear
- Logical structure is hard to follow
- Reader must fill in significant gaps
- Feels like an outline rather than a complete response

### Score 2: Failed
- Fundamentally incomplete; major sections missing
- No clear structure or logical progression
- Cannot be understood as a standalone response
- Requires substantial restructuring

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Missing context section** — jumps into details without establishing what problem is being solved
2. **No conclusion** — ends abruptly without synthesis or recommendation
3. **Orphaned paragraphs** — isolated claims that don't connect to surrounding sections
4. **Wrong progression** — detailed examples before explaining the concept

### Repair Protocol
1. **Audit against template:** Does this content type have a standard structure? (Problem → Solution → Evidence → Implementation → Conclusion)
2. **Add missing sections:** Identify the most critical gap and add a placeholder, then flesh it out
3. **Verify flow:** Read only the section headers. Do they tell a coherent story?
4. **Check for islands:** Mark each paragraph with the section it belongs to. Are there orphans?
5. **Strengthen entry/exit:** Revise introduction to set up what follows; revise conclusion to tie it together

## 2. Veracidad (Truthfulness/Factual Accuracy)

**Measures:** Are all factual claims accurate, verifiable, and supported? Are there unsupported claims or weasel words?

### Score 10: Excellence
- Every factual claim is verifiable and correct
- Claims are sourced or framed appropriately ("According to...", "Research shows...")
- No weasel words ("somehow", "mysteriously", "it is believed")
- Speculative statements are labeled as such ("Likely...", "Could result in...")
- Numbers and dates are precise and cited

### Score 8: Good
- All major claims are accurate
- Minor claims lack sources but are defensible
- Weasel words present but minimal (1-2)
- Speculative sections are appropriately flagged

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Most claims are accurate; some lack verification
- Weasel words present (3-5) but don't undermine credibility
- Mix of sourced and unsourced claims
- Speculative nature of some claims is unclear

### Score 4: Weak
- Several factual claims lack support or are dubious
- Weasel words frequent (6+)
- Reader cannot easily verify key claims
- Speculation presented as fact in places

### Score 2: Failed
- Multiple false or misleading claims
- Heavy reliance on weasel words and unsupported assertions
- Reader cannot trust the content
- No distinction between fact and speculation

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Citation gaps** — makes specific claims without citing sources ("Studies show X" without naming the study)
2. **Weasel word accumulation** — "It is often said that...", "Many believe...", "Somehow, mysteriously..."
3. **Speculative statements as fact** — "Will happen" instead of "Could happen"
4. **Outdated information** — cites old statistics without noting recency

### Repair Protocol
1. **Audit every factual claim:** Mark each claim that isn't pure opinion. List how you'd verify it.
2. **Eliminate weasel words:** Search for: "somehow", "mysteriously", "often", "many believe", "it is said", "appears to", "seems to"
3. **Add sources:** For any claim a skeptical reader would challenge, add source or qualification
4. **Clarify speculation:** Prefix speculative claims with "Likely...", "Could...", "May result in..."
5. **Date-check numbers:** Verify that statistics are recent or note their age ("As of 2024...")

## 3. Calidad (Quality/Writing Quality)

**Measures:** Is the writing professional, precise, and free of filler? Does every sentence carry meaning?

### Score 10: Excellence
- Every sentence has clear purpose and meaning
- No filler words or sentences
- Active voice dominates (80%+ of sentences)
- Vocabulary is precise and appropriate
- No grammatical errors or typos
- Register is consistent throughout

### Score 8: Good
- Sentences are mostly purposeful; 1-2 minor fillers
- Active voice ~70%
- Vocabulary is precise
- Clean grammar and spelling
- Consistent register

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Some filler sentences; readers notice them
- Active voice ~60%
- Vocabulary mostly appropriate; some imprecision
- Few grammatical errors
- Register mostly consistent

### Score 4: Weak
- Multiple filler sentences; clutters meaning
- Active voice ~50%
- Vague or imprecise vocabulary
- Grammar errors detract from understanding
- Register shifts are noticeable

### Score 2: Failed
- Sentence-level clarity is poor
- Heavy passive voice and filler
- Vocabulary is vague or incorrect
- Grammar errors frequently distract
- Register is inconsistent

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Passive voice dominance** — "It is believed that X should be done" instead of "You should do X"
2. **Filler phrases** — "In this day and age", "It goes without saying", "Rather interesting", "As a matter of fact"
3. **Redundancy** — restates the same point in consecutive sentences
4. **Register inconsistency** — shifts from formal to casual unexpectedly

### Repair Protocol
1. **Filler audit:** Read for phrases that add no meaning. Delete them.
2. **Voice conversion:** Find every "to be" + past participle. Rewrite active: "This was discovered by researchers" → "Researchers discovered this"
3. **Precision pass:** Replace vague words with specific ones: "good" → "efficient", "interesting" → "revealing", "thing" → [specific noun]
4. **Register check:** Read aloud. Does the formality level match the audience?
5. **Sentence weight:** Does each sentence carry its own weight? If two sentences say similar things, merge them.

## 4. Densidad (Density/Information Density)

**Measures:** Is information packed efficiently? Maximum value per word?

### Score 10: Excellence
- No redundant sentences or concepts
- Can cut 20% and lose nothing important
- Eliminates duplicates; merges related ideas
- Tables, lists, and structured formats used where appropriate
- Every paragraph advances the argument

### Score 8: Good
- Minimal redundancy; could cut ~10%
- Most concepts are distinct
- Good use of structure and formatting
- Paragraphs are focused

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Some repetition; could cut ~15%
- A few redundant sentences
- Could use more structured formats (lists, tables)
- Paragraphs have extraneous material

### Score 4: Weak
- Significant repetition; could cut ~25%
- Multiple concepts restated across sections
- Poor use of formatting; reads like prose when structure would help
- Paragraphs meander

### Score 2: Failed
- Heavy redundancy; could cut ~40%+
- Same point made multiple times
- No structured formatting despite opportunity
- Reads as filler

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Concept repetition** — states the same point in the intro, body, and conclusion
2. **Sentence redundancy** — "X is important. X matters. X is significant."
3. **Format choice** — information presented as prose when a table would be clearer
4. **Exemplar overload** — 5 examples for a single point when 1-2 suffice

### Repair Protocol
1. **2x density constraint:** If a paragraph has 2 sentences saying the same thing, merge them
2. **Table conversion:** List 3+ related items in prose? Convert to table or list
3. **Filler removal:** Mark sentences that repeat the immediately preceding sentence. Delete
4. **Deduplication:** Highlight the core claim. If it appears in multiple sections, consolidate
5. **Structure audit:** Skim the first sentence of each paragraph. Do they form a coherent outline?

## 5. Simplicidad (Simplicity/Accessibility)

**Measures:** Could a non-expert follow the structure? Is jargon minimized and explained?

### Score 10: Excellence
- Structure is self-evident; could be followed by someone unfamiliar with the domain
- Jargon is absent or clearly explained on first use
- Heading hierarchy is shallow (max 3 levels)
- Concepts build logically from simple to complex
- Examples use familiar references

### Score 8: Good
- Structure is clear with minimal domain knowledge
- Most jargon is explained
- Heading depth is mostly shallow (some 4-level sections)
- Progression from simple to complex is mostly clear

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Structure requires some domain knowledge
- Jargon present; explanations incomplete or unclear
- Heading depth occasionally goes to 4-5 levels
- Progression could be more gradual

### Score 4: Weak
- Structure assumes significant domain knowledge
- Jargon frequent and poorly explained
- Deep heading hierarchy (5+ levels)
- Jumps between difficulty levels

### Score 2: Failed
- Structure is incomprehensible without expert background
- Heavy jargon with no explanations
- Chaotic heading structure
- No progression; scattered complexity

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Unexplained jargon** — uses domain-specific terms without definition
2. **Deep nesting** — heading hierarchy goes to 6+ levels (# ## ### #### ##### ###### #######)
3. **Assumed knowledge** — skips foundational concepts
4. **Complex examples first** — uses sophisticated examples before explaining basics

### Repair Protocol
1. **Flatten hierarchy:** If you have more than 3 heading levels, restructure into parallel sections
2. **Jargon inventory:** List all domain-specific terms. Add brief explanations (inline or in glossary) for non-obvious ones
3. **Gradient test:** Skim first sentences of sections. Does difficulty increase gradually or jump?
4. **Example reordering:** Start with the simplest example. Progress to complex
5. **Audience reframe:** Rewrite one section as if explaining to someone in a different field

## 6. Claridad (Clarity/Unambiguity)

**Measures:** One interpretation only? Could a pedantic reader misunderstand?

### Score 10: Excellence
- No ambiguous phrasing
- Pronouns have single, clear antecedents
- Vague qualifiers absent ("appropriate", "significant", "sometimes")
- Conditions are explicit ("when X happens, do Y")
- No dangling modifiers

### Score 8: Good
- Minimal ambiguity; meaning is clear in context
- Most pronouns are clear
- Few vague qualifiers (1-2)
- Most conditions are explicit

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Meaning is generally clear but could be sharpened
- Some pronouns have multiple possible antecedents (context resolves it)
- Several vague qualifiers (3-5)
- Some implicit conditions

### Score 4: Weak
- Meaning requires inference from context
- Pronouns are ambiguous
- Vague qualifiers frequent (6+)
- Conditions are unclear or implicit

### Score 2: Failed
- Multiple interpretations are possible
- Pronouns lack clear antecedents
- Heavy vague qualifier use
- Conditions are missing entirely

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Vague qualifiers** — "appropriate", "reasonable", "significant", "sometimes", "often", "usually", "typically"
2. **Pronoun ambiguity** — "X and Y both affect Z. It increases when it rises" (which "it"?)
3. **Implicit conditions** — "This works well" (in what situation?)
4. **Dangling modifiers** — "While running the test, the server crashed" (server was running the test?)

### Repair Protocol
1. **Vague qualifier inventory:** Find every instance of: appropriate, reasonable, significant, sometimes, often, usually, typical, roughly, somewhat, perhaps
2. **Replace with specifics:** Build a replacement table:
   - "appropriate" → "meeting [explicit criteria]"
   - "sometimes" → "when [condition]"
   - "significant" → "[X]% increase" or "[measurable change]"
3. **Pronoun audit:** Mark every pronoun. Trace it to its noun. If multiple options exist, replace pronoun with noun
4. **Condition audit:** Every imperative sentence ("do X") should have "when" or "in case of" nearby
5. **Modifier check:** Read modifying phrases. Is the actor clear? Reorder if needed

## 7. Precisión (Precision/Specificity)

**Measures:** Are constraints and claims specific enough to act on? Aspirational vs. testable?

### Score 10: Excellence
- All constraints are testable (measurable, observable, verifiable)
- No aspirational statements without specifics
- Numbers have units and context
- "Good" is defined with criteria
- Recommendations have success metrics

### Score 8: Good
- Most constraints are testable
- Minimal aspirational statements; mostly have supporting specifics
- Numbers usually include units
- Most "good" definitions are clear

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Some constraints are vague or aspirational
- Mix of testable and aspirational claims
- Numbers sometimes lack units
- "Good" is sometimes undefined

### Score 4: Weak
- Many constraints are aspirational or unclear
- Vague claims dominate ("be concise", "make it better")
- Numbers lack context
- Success metrics are undefined

### Score 2: Failed
- Almost entirely aspirational
- No testable constraints
- Numbers lack units or context
- Impossible to measure success

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Aspirational statements** — "be concise", "write clearly", "improve performance", "make it better" (without defining what that means)
2. **Unit-less numbers** — "under 300" (what? words? tokens? seconds?)
3. **Undefined quality terms** — "good code", "effective communication", "strong argument" (what makes it good?)
4. **Missing success criteria** — "implement X" (how do you know it worked?)

### Repair Protocol
1. **Aspirational audit:** Find every bare imperative ("be X", "make Y", "improve Z"). Replace with testable version
2. **Conversion patterns:**
   - "be concise" → "maximum 300 words"
   - "make it better" → "improve [metric] by [amount]"
   - "write clearly" → "[metric specific to clarity, e.g., max 2 vague qualifiers]"
   - "good performance" → "[specific latency/throughput target]"
3. **Unit audit:** Every number should have units and context
4. **Success criteria:** For each major claim, add "Success looks like: [testable description]"
5. **Threshold setting:** Replace "many", "few", "several" with numbers

## 8. Profundidad (Depth/Edge Case Coverage)

**Measures:** Does it handle non-obvious cases? What happens with unexpected input?

### Score 10: Excellence
- Edge cases are identified and handled
- Failure modes are anticipated and addressed
- Works with incomplete, contradictory, or extreme data
- Limitations are explicit
- Fallback strategies provided

### Score 8: Good
- Most edge cases covered
- Some failure modes addressed
- Mostly handles unexpected input
- Limitations are mentioned
- Some fallbacks provided

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Some edge cases considered
- Few failure modes mentioned
- Main path works; edge cases less clear
- Limitations mentioned but vague
- Limited fallbacks

### Score 4: Weak
- Edge cases largely ignored
- Failure modes not anticipated
- Main path only
- Limitations not mentioned
- No fallbacks

### Score 2: Failed
- Only happy path considered
- Will break on normal variations
- No mention of limitations
- No fallback strategies

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Happy path only** — works perfectly when everything goes as planned; breaks when input is incomplete, contradictory, or extreme
2. **Missing error handling** — no mention of what happens when assumptions fail
3. **Unaddressed ambiguity** — "If two interpretations are possible..." (what then?)
4. **Scaling blindness** — assumes small dataset; breaks at scale

### Repair Protocol
1. **Edge case discovery protocol:** Ask for each component:
   - What if this data is missing?
   - What if this data is contradictory?
   - What if this is much larger/smaller than expected?
   - What if the user doesn't follow the path?
   - What if two conditions apply simultaneously?
2. **Failure mode list:** Identify top 5 ways this could fail. For each: what happens? Is there a fallback?
3. **Limitation audit:** What assumptions does this make? List them explicitly
4. **Fallback strategies:** For each likely failure, propose a fallback or workaround
5. **Test the extremes:** Does this work with minimal input? Maximum input? Contradictory input?

## 9. Coherencia (Coherence/Internal Consistency)

**Measures:** Do all sections reinforce each other? Are there contradictions?

### Score 10: Excellence
- No internal contradictions
- Cross-references are accurate and helpful
- Claims in one section support claims in another
- Terminology is consistent throughout
- Logical dependencies between sections are clear

### Score 8: Good
- No major contradictions
- Cross-references mostly accurate
- Sections mostly reinforce each other
- Terminology mostly consistent
- Dependencies mostly clear

### Score 6: Acceptable
- No direct contradictions but some tension
- Cross-references occasionally unclear or missing
- Some sections seem disconnected
- Terminology mostly consistent with minor variations
- Dependencies sometimes unclear

### Score 4: Weak
- Apparent contradictions exist
- Cross-references are missing or inaccurate
- Sections feel disconnected
- Terminology shifts
- Dependencies are unclear

### Score 2: Failed
- Clear contradictions
- No cross-references
- Sections are disconnected or contradictory
- Terminology is inconsistent
- No apparent logical structure

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Contradictory claims** — Section A says "always do X", Section B says "avoid X in some cases" without reconciliation
2. **Broken references** — "See Section 3" but Section 3 doesn't address the topic
3. **Terminology drift** — uses "user" and "customer" interchangeably without clarification
4. **Orphaned sections** — a section doesn't connect to the overall argument

### Repair Protocol
1. **Cross-section dependency map:** For each section, list what it depends on (what must be established first). Do those sections come before it?
2. **Terminology audit:** List key terms. Ensure they're used consistently. If a term has multiple meanings, clarify
3. **Contradiction check:** For each major claim, find where it could conflict with other claims. Reconcile
4. **Cross-reference validation:** Find every reference to another section. Verify it's accurate and helpful
5. **Coherence test:** Shuffle section order. What's the minimum ordering needed for the argument to flow?

## 10. Valor (Value/User Impact)

**Measures:** Meaningful improvement over not having this? Would the user pay for this quality?

### Score 10: Excellence
- Applies domain expertise not obvious to the user
- Provides methodology or reasoning, not just facts
- Actionable with clear next steps
- Addresses user's real problem, not generic solution
- Exceeds user expectations

### Score 8: Good
- Shows expertise in places
- Mostly provides reasoning
- Actionable with minor gaps
- Relevant to user's problem
- Meets expectations

### Score 6: Acceptable
- Standard expertise level
- Mix of facts and reasoning
- Actionable but requires interpretation
- Relevant to stated problem
- Meets minimal expectations

### Score 4: Weak
- Limited expertise; generic content
- Mostly facts; limited reasoning
- Requires significant interpretation
- Tangentially relevant
- Below expectations

### Score 2: Failed
- No domain expertise; generic
- Only facts, no guidance
- Not actionable
- Misses user's actual need
- No value over generic search result

### Common Failure Patterns
1. **Generic response** — could apply to anyone; doesn't leverage domain knowledge
2. **Missing methodology** — states facts without explaining why or how
3. **No reasoning directives** — doesn't help the user think through the problem
4. **Surface-level only** — obvious information; no insights
5. **Wrong problem solved** — addresses stated question but not underlying need

### Repair Protocol
1. **Expertise injection:** What can an expert add that a generic answer can't? Add it
   - Domain-specific frameworks
   - Common pitfalls unique to this domain
   - Shortcuts or best practices from experience
   - Reasoning patterns specific to the field
2. **Methodology audit:** For each recommendation, can you explain why? Add the "why"
3. **Reasoning directives:** Add guidance on how to think about this: "First, assess X. Then consider Y"
4. **Insight layer:** What's non-obvious? What do experts know that novices don't? Add it
5. **User problem check:** Is the user asking about X, but actually needing help with Y? Reframe to address the real problem

---

## Summary: Repair Priority

When you need to improve a response quickly:

1. **Start with lowest score:** Fix the criterion with the worst score first (highest impact per effort)
2. **Quick wins:** Densidad, Claridad, Precisión (often fixable in 1 pass)
3. **Medium effort:** Calidad, Simplicidad (require rewriting passages)
4. **High effort:** Fundamento, Profundidad, Valor (may require structural changes)
5. **Late stage:** Coherencia, Veracidad (audit after other improvements, verify consistency)

---

## When in Doubt

- **Ask a pedantic reader:** Could someone deliberately misinterpret this?
- **Read aloud:** Does every sentence add meaning?
- **Teach it to a novice:** Can they follow the structure without your explanation?
- **Check for contradictions:** Find two related claims. Do they align?
- **Verify the value:** Would you pay for this information? Why?
