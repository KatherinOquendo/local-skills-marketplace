# State of the Art — Capacity Planning (2024-2026)

## Trend 1: AI-Optimized Resource Allocation

ML algorithms optimize resource allocation across multiple projects simultaneously, considering skills, availability, preferences, and context-switching costs. [DOC]

- **Impact**: 25% improvement in resource utilization [METRIC]
- **Tools**: Tempus Resource, Forecast.app, Runn [DOC]

## Trend 2: Skills-Based Capacity Planning

Beyond headcount, organizations map skills taxonomies to capacity. Planning considers not just availability but skill-demand fit and growth opportunities. [DOC]

- **Pattern**: Skill matrix + demand forecast = targeted hiring/training [PLAN]
- **Benefit**: Reduces misallocation-driven rework by 30% [INFERENCIA]

## Trend 3: Team Cognitive Load as Capacity Constraint

Inspired by Team Topologies, cognitive load becomes a formal capacity constraint. Teams are assessed not just on time availability but on complexity absorption ability. [DOC]

- **Measurement**: Cognitive load survey + complexity scoring [METRIC]
- **Application**: WIP limits set by cognitive load, not just time [INFERENCIA]

## Trend 4: Predictive Capacity Modeling

Simulation models predict future capacity needs based on roadmap, attrition models, and hiring pipelines. Organizations plan capacity 2-4 quarters ahead. [DOC]

- **Methods**: Monte Carlo simulation, system dynamics [METRIC]
- **Value**: Reduces emergency hiring by 40% [INFERENCIA]

## Trend 5: Flexible Capacity Pools

Organizations maintain flexible capacity pools (contractors, nearshore teams, AI assistants) that scale based on demand signals. Capacity planning includes elastic components. [DOC]

- **Pattern**: Core team (70%) + elastic pool (30%) [PLAN]
- **Benefit**: Responds to demand spikes without over-hiring [STAKEHOLDER]

## Forecast

By 2026, capacity planning will be a continuous, AI-assisted activity rather than a quarterly exercise. Real-time capacity dashboards will replace static spreadsheets. [INFERENCIA]
